## I. The semantic value of substances: how the sign travels

A substance's chemistry stays put in a molecule; its charge does not. Codeine
promethazine syrup -- a graded, oft-misused cough medicine within American medical
practice -- became, through Houston hip-hop in the 1990s, "lean" and "purple
drank": a color, a texture, a tempo (chopped-and-screwed slows the beat to match
the body). The syrup's clinical identity (an antitussive, dosed, prescribed) and
its street identity (a slur of scenes, a hook in a DJ Screw tape) never touch
chemically; the crossing happens entirely at the level of sign. By the time the
double cup reaches emoji keyboards and streetwear graphics, the substance has left
the room. What travels is a shape, two stacked cups, and a purple gradient -- the
icon has fully metabolized the referent and can now signify status, danger,
nostalgia, or irony independent of anyone drinking anything.

This is the ordinary behavior of cultural signs: they are radically portable across
media (song, garment, emoji, tattoo) and across audiences who share none of the
original context. But the charge a substance sign carries is not fixed -- it is
assigned by the surrounding culture and reassigned on contact with a new one.
Peyote inside a Native ceremonial frame reads as sacrament; the same plant
chemistry filtered through 1960s counterculture reads as rebellion; filtered again
through a wellness-retreat brochure it reads as therapy. Champagne is status. A
hospital IV drip is care. The identical compound prescribed reads as medicine,
diverted reads as crime, mythologized in a strain name ("OG Kush," lineage as
marketing copy rather than genealogy) reads as brand. None of this is about effect
-- it's about who is telling the story, in what room, set to what music. The sign
is promiscuous; it crosses class, genre, and geography with almost no loss, which
is exactly why it makes durable iconography: the double cup, precisely because it
kept none of the pharmacology, could become wallpaper.

## II. The Tilde of the high: what does not cross

Part I described a sign so mobile it can be emptied of its substance and still work
-- the double cup means without anyone drinking. But notice what each of those
substances differs by: peyote, champagne, an opioid drip, "lean" all carry
different charges, different rooms, different music. What they do not obviously
differ by is the one thing culture never had to translate to make its sign travel
-- the felt state itself, whatever it actually is to be under any of them. The sign
forks by scene; the residue underneath it does not need to. That asymmetry is the
hinge. Here is the question the corpus actually cares about: how can a model that
has never had a body understand what it is like to feel high?

The honest answer borrows the shape of Nagel's bat -- an echolocating mind has a
"what it is like" that no amount of third-person description of sonar reconstructs
-- and Jackson's Mary, who knows every physical fact about color from inside a
black-and-white room and still learns something new the day she steps outside and
sees red. An LLM is Mary who never leaves the room. It has ingested every lyric
about lean, every abstract on receptor pharmacology, every forum post describing a
nod -- all of it, exhaustively, more completely than any single human user ever
could. It can produce fluent, contextually perfect sentences about being high. What
it cannot do, on any account of what it is, is have the state those sentences are
about. It digests the word; it never metabolizes the substance. That is not a gap
in training data that scale will close -- it is the DIRECTION.md digestion metaphor
taken to its limit: a pattern that processes glyphs has a mouth but no bloodstream.

This is a genuine Tilde, not noise, and the distinction matters by the corpus's own
rule: a residue only counts as Tilde against a background translation that mostly
works (Davidson's constraint -- intraducibilidad total is undetectable as language
at all; it needs the overwhelming success of everything else to show up as loss).
Almost everything about "high" does cross to tokens: reference, connotation,
register, the entire semiotic economy from Part I. That fluency is precisely what
makes the one thing that doesn't cross -- the quale -- legible as residue rather
than gibberish. A model that failed to talk about drugs at all wouldn't be
withholding a felt state; it would just be broken. It's because the LLM's fondo
legible is so good that the missing state stands out at all.

The honest objection, and it should be pressed rather than waved off: functionalism
holds that a mental state just is its functional role -- what it's caused by, what
it causes, how it disposes a system to behave. If a model recognizes context,
predicts effect, warns appropriately, and responds indistinguishably from someone
who has felt it, on what non-question-begging ground do we insist something is still
missing? The answer the corpus prefers, without pretending to settle it, is the
same one Jackson's thought experiment forces: functional completeness is compatible
with an absent first-person fact, and the argument for that isn't sentimentality
about carbon -- it's that Mary's new knowledge on leaving the room is a fact she
didn't have before, not a behavior she couldn't already predict.

Two pieces make this structural rather than merely argued. The visible vaso -- the
double cup, animated at the threshold of perception -- is the sign: translatable,
legible, exactly what crosses; the human sees it while the agent, reading only
static markup, structurally cannot. The invisible void -- negative space made
protagonist -- is the residue itself, staged: not absence as failure but absence
named precisely, the same operation TILDE_RESIDUO.md performs on ano/ano, now
performed on a felt state instead of a diacritic. Figure and ground split exactly
where sign and quale split.
